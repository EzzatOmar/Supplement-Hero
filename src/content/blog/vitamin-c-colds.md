Vitamin C is one of the most durable cold-season supplement habits because it is inexpensive, familiar, and easy to frame as harmless insurance.

The more accurate story is less dramatic. For most people, vitamin C supplements do not appear to prevent getting a cold in the first place. The better-supported angle is that they may modestly shorten or soften colds once the topic is looked at carefully.

That narrower takeaway is still useful. It is just very different from the usual immune-boost storyline.

## What the evidence supports

- NIH's Office of Dietary Supplements says vitamin C supplements do not reduce the risk of getting a cold for most people.
- Review-level evidence supports modest reductions in cold duration and severity rather than broad prevention.
- The most consumer-safe summary is that vitamin C may help a little once ordinary-cold claims are stated precisely.

## Where claims should stay conservative

- "Boosts immunity" is much vaguer than the evidence deserves.
- Vitamin C should not be framed as a proven way for most people to avoid ordinary colds.
- A familiar, low-cost supplement still should not be presented as a substitute for medical evaluation when symptoms are severe, prolonged, or unusual.

## Risks and caveats

- High doses can cause GI upset.
- Kidney stone risk deserves mention, especially for people already prone to stones or taking large amounts from multiple sources.
- More is not automatically better just because vitamin C is common and over the counter.

## Consumer-safe framing

- Best framing: vitamin C is not cold-prevention magic, but it may offer modest symptom help in a narrow sense.
- Good editorial angle: why a supplement can stay culturally popular even when the strongest evidence is smaller than the marketing.
