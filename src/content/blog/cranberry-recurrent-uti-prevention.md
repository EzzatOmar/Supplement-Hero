Cranberry is one of those supplement topics where a small useful claim often gets stretched into a much bigger one. The stronger consumer takeaway is narrower than the average label or juice ad implies.

The evidence-backed angle is prevention in selected people with recurrent urinary tract infections. That is not the same thing as treating an active infection, and it is not a license to treat every urinary symptom like a cranberry problem.

That distinction matters because recurrent UTI prevention and active UTI care are different conversations.

## What the evidence supports

- Review-level evidence, including a Cochrane review summarized via PubMed, supports cranberry products as a way to reduce recurrent symptomatic UTIs in selected populations.
- The most defensible consumer framing is risk reduction for recurrence, not treatment of an infection that is already happening.
- A narrower prevention claim is more accurate than broad urinary-wellness marketing.

## Where claims should stay conservative

- Cranberry should not be sold as a treatment for an active UTI.
- Urinary burning, fever, flank pain, or worsening symptoms should not be treated like a routine supplement-shopping problem.
- Cranberry juice drinks and standardized supplement products should not be assumed to behave the same way.

## Risks and caveats

- GI upset is possible.
- Kidney stone concerns matter for some people.
- Cranberry can interact with warfarin, which makes medication context part of the main safety story.

## Consumer-safe framing

- Best framing: cranberry may help reduce recurrent UTI risk in some groups, but it is not a treatment for an active infection.
- Good editorial angle: why one of the biggest supplement mistakes is confusing prevention support with treatment.
