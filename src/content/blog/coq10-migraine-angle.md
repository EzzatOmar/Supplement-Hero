CoQ10 is easier to write about responsibly when the article stops trying to make it sound like a supplement for everything that matters with age.

The sharper evidence story is migraine. That is a cleaner editorial angle than the broad heart-health, anti-aging, and statin-related claims that tend to dominate product pages and social posts.

This distinction matters because a supplement can have a plausible niche without earning the much bigger identity that marketing tries to give it.

## What the evidence supports

- NCCIH says there is limited evidence that CoQ10 may help reduce migraine frequency and duration.
- The broader cardiovascular story is less settled, which makes migraine the more defensible consumer angle.
- The best plain-language summary is that CoQ10 may have a narrower preventive role in headaches, but that should not be confused with proof of major whole-body benefits.

## Where claims should stay conservative

- CoQ10 should not be sold as a proven anti-aging supplement or a broad heart-health solution.
- The evidence does not justify large promises about statin muscle pain, major blood-pressure effects, or sweeping energy benefits.
- A migraine-related discussion should stay separate from the much louder claims often used to market CoQ10 capsules to almost everyone over a certain age.

## Risks and caveats

- CoQ10 can interact with warfarin, insulin, and some cancer treatments, so medication context matters.
- A limited evidence signal is not the same thing as a recommendation for every reader with headaches.
- Categories built around "cellular energy" language often sound more established than the actual outcome evidence supports.

## Consumer-safe framing

- Best framing: CoQ10 is more credible as a migraine-focused supplement discussion than as a general longevity product.
- Good editorial angle: why the cleanest use case for CoQ10 is smaller and less glamorous than the marketing campaign around it.
