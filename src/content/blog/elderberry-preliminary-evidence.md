Elderberry has become one of the most recognizable cold-season supplements because the story is easy to sell: traditional use, a dark berry, and lots of language about immunity. The evidence does not support that level of confidence.

The more accurate consumer view is that only a small number of studies exist, and the signal is preliminary. That leaves elderberry in a very different category from the stronger prevention and antiviral claims that often appear in marketing.

That matters because people often translate "might help symptoms a bit" into "proven to prevent illness."

## What the evidence supports

- NCCIH says only a small number of elderberry studies exist.
- Preliminary research suggests possible symptom relief for upper respiratory infections.
- The most defensible takeaway is that elderberry remains an early-evidence symptom topic, not a proven prevention strategy.

## Where claims should stay conservative

- Elderberry should not be sold as a proven way to prevent flu, COVID-19, or ordinary colds.
- It should not be framed as a replacement for vaccination or standard care.
- Traditional-use storytelling should not be treated as a substitute for strong clinical evidence.

## Risks and caveats

- Raw or unripe elderberry and other parts of the plant can be toxic.
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding, and medication-interaction data are limited.
- Safety uncertainty belongs near the top of the conversation, not buried under immunity branding.

## Consumer-safe framing

- Best framing: elderberry is a popular supplement with preliminary symptom evidence, not a proven antiviral shield.
- Good editorial angle: why a supplement can dominate seasonal marketing long before the clinical case becomes convincing.
